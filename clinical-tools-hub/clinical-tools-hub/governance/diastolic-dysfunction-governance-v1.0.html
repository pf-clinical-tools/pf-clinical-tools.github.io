Diastolic Dysfunction Bedside Decision Support Tool

A multiparametric, conservative framework for echocardiographic interpretation

Status: Complete locked fallback governance document (v1.0).
This page is provided for governance, transparency, and audit. It does not replace clinical judgement or formal echocardiography reporting.

1. Introduction

Diastolic dysfunction is a major contributor to symptoms of heart failure in patients with preserved or mildly reduced left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF). Impaired myocardial relaxation, reduced ventricular compliance, and abnormal ventricular–atrial coupling result in elevation of left ventricular filling pressures, leading to exertional dyspnoea, pulmonary congestion, and exercise intolerance despite apparently preserved systolic function. Echocardiography is the principal non-invasive modality for evaluating these processes; however, interpretation of diastolic function is recognised to be complex, context-dependent, and subject to substantial inter-observer variability.¹–³

International guideline recommendations emphasise that no single echocardiographic parameter reliably reflects left ventricular filling pressure across all clinical contexts.¹,⁴ Instead, a multiparametric approach integrating concordant findings is required. In routine clinical practice, echocardiographic datasets are frequently incomplete, qualitative, or discordant, making bedside interpretation challenging for non-specialists. Over-simplification risks false certainty, while excessive technical detail may impair clinical usability.

The Diastolic Dysfunction Bedside Decision Support Tool was developed to address this gap. It does not redefine diagnostic criteria; rather, it provides a transparent, conservative, and explainable framework for integrating echocardiographic data, explicitly acknowledging uncertainty, missing information, and the physiological context in which measurements were acquired.

2. Purpose of the Tool

The purpose of the tool is to:

  • Support structured interpretation of echocardiographic parameters relevant to diastolic function
  • Estimate resting left atrial pressure (LAP) physiology using a conservative multiparametric approach
  • Report diastolic grade only where grading is methodologically appropriate
  • Provide an explicit degree of confidence based on data quality, concordance, and acquisition context

The tool is not intended to:

  • Diagnose heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF)
  • Replace formal echocardiography reporting
  • Substitute for invasive haemodynamic assessment
  • Mandate or automate clinical management decisions

Clinical judgement and contextual assessment remain essential.

3. Intended Audience and Scope of Use

Intended audience

  • Cardiologists and heart failure specialists
  • Acute and general physicians
  • Advanced echocardiography practitioners and physiologists
  • Clinicians participating in multidisciplinary team (MDT) discussions

Intended settings

  • Inpatient wards
  • Outpatient clinics
  • MDT review and longitudinal patient assessment

Scope clarification (critical)

This tool does not guide echocardiographic image acquisition or measurement. It supports interpretation only of echocardiographic parameters that have already been acquired, validated, and formally reported by appropriately trained practitioners.

Importantly, scope exclusions relate to the physiological context at the time echocardiographic parameters were acquired, not to the patient’s current clinical state.

4. Clinical and Echocardiographic Context

Diastolic dysfunction reflects abnormalities in myocardial relaxation, passive stiffness, and ventricular–atrial interaction. These processes lead to elevation of filling pressures initially during exertion and, with disease progression, at rest. Chronic elevation of LAP results in left atrial remodelling, pulmonary venous hypertension, and secondary pulmonary vascular changes.⁵–⁷

Echocardiographic indices of diastolic function are indirect surrogates of these processes and are influenced by loading conditions, heart rate, rhythm, vasoactive therapy, and ventilatory support. Consequently, guideline statements consistently emphasise corroboration across multiple parameters and caution against interpretation during acute haemodynamic perturbation.¹,⁴

5. Echocardiographic Assessment of Diastolic Function

Standard echocardiographic assessment includes transmitral inflow velocities, tissue Doppler indices, left atrial size and function, and pulmonary pressure surrogates.

6. Tool Design and Logic Framework

The tool applies a hierarchical, multiparametric logic prioritising physiological plausibility and protection against over-classification.

Design principles

  • Conservative inference
  • Requirement for corroboration
  • Explicit handling of missing or qualitative data
  • Full explainability of outputs

Table 3. Parameter classification

Category Parameters Role
Strong pressure markers E/e′, TR/PASP, LAVI, LA strain Primary determinants
Supportive markers DT, extreme E/A Cannot act alone
Qualitative surrogates “Restrictive pattern reported” Require corroboration

Table 4. Minimum data thresholds

Context Requirement
Sinus rhythm ≥2 strong pressure markers
Atrial fibrillation ≥2 AF-specific markers
Grading E velocity + E/A (or restrictive pattern)

Failure to meet these thresholds results in an Indeterminate output by design.

Methods clarification (inserted):
Where both septal and lateral e′ velocities are available, the tool calculates an averaged e′ value for derivation of E/e′; if one or both e′ measurements are absent, E/e′ is not derived and the tool proceeds using alternative independent pressure markers with appropriate downgrading of confidence.

7. Special Populations and Constraints

Formal diastolic grading is unreliable in:

  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Significant mitral valve disease or prior repair
  • Pacing rhythms
  • Tachycardia with E–A fusion

In these settings, the tool suppresses or de-emphasises grading and focuses on LAP estimation, consistent with guideline cautions.¹,⁸

8. Output Structure and Interpretation

The tool produces three outputs:

  1. Estimated resting LAP (Normal / Increased / Indeterminate)
  2. Diastolic grade, where appropriate
  3. Narrative mini-report summarising inputs and reasoning

Table 5. Output interpretation

Output Meaning Clinical implication
Normal LAP No evidence of elevated pressure Consider non-cardiac causes
Increased LAP Likely elevated filling pressure Supports HF physiology
Indeterminate Insufficient or discordant data Further assessment required

“Indeterminate” is a protective outcome, not a failure.

9. Confidence Scoring and Explainability

Rather than binary conclusions, the tool assigns a confidence level based on:

  • Data quality
  • Rhythm suitability
  • Parameter concordance
  • Completeness of information

Table 6. Confidence levels

Confidence Characteristics
High Concordant markers, good data quality
Moderate Partial concordance or recognised limitations
Low Missing data, poor quality, major constraints

This approach replaces false precision with transparent uncertainty.

10. Why Sensitivity and Specificity Are Not Applicable

Diastolic dysfunction lacks a single gold-standard comparator. Invasive haemodynamics are context-dependent and not routinely available, while echocardiographic parameters are probabilistic and heterogeneous.¹,⁹ Traditional diagnostic accuracy metrics are therefore inappropriate for this class of interpretive tool.

Instead, the tool prioritises explainability, uncertainty handling, and conservative inference, with confidence scoring reflecting real-world clinical reasoning.

11. Clinical Limitations and Acquisition Context

Patients may have underlying diastolic dysfunction during acute haemodynamic instability or shock. However, echocardiographic parameters acquired during such states are often profoundly influenced by transient loading conditions, vasoactive therapy, heart rate, and ventilatory support, and may not reliably reflect resting diastolic physiology.

Accordingly, echocardiographic parameters obtained during acute haemodynamic instability should not be interpreted using this tool. Previously acquired echocardiographic data obtained under stable physiological conditions may be interpreted, even if the patient is currently unstable, provided those data remain clinically relevant.

12. Governance, Safety, and Version Control

The tool is versioned, archived, and updated conservatively in alignment with evolving evidence and guideline recommendations. Prior validated versions are preserved. The design aligns with digital clinical safety and governance principles.

13. Disclaimer

This tool is a clinical decision-support aid. It does not replace clinician judgement, formal echocardiography reporting, or invasive assessment where indicated. Responsibility for clinical decisions remains with the treating clinician.
